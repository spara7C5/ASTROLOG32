In Canada, time-zones span the country east-to-west:

  • Newfoundland, (Gander, St Johns) is U.T.-3.30hrs.
  • New Brunswick is U.T.-4, as is Nova Scotia (e.g.Halifax).
  • Eastern Ontario (e.g Toronto, Ottawa, i.e. east of 60W) and Quebec (e.g Montreal) use the US's EST, UT-5.
  • Manitoba (Winnepeg), west Ontario (Thunder Bay), and the S.E. corner of Saskatchewan match CST, UT-6.
  • Alberta (Calgary, Edmonton), with most of NorthWest Terr., and most of Saskatchewan (see above), keep the US's MST, UT-7. In 1960, NorthWest Terr. used 5 zones: E of W, 68W, UT-4; 68W to 85W, UT-5; 85W to 102W, UT-6; 102W to 120W, UT-7; 120W westward, UT-8.
  • British Columbia, mostly, (e.g Vancouver), and Yukon match PST, UT-8.
Before 1906, no zones applied and LMT should be used.
There are many inconsistencies by individual city or town: see [DST].
Saskatchewan alone does not apply a summertime shift.

United States has five onshore time-zones, all with [DST], except Arizona in the UT-7 zone, which had DST in only one year - 1967.

  • Eastern Standard, (EST), UT-5; States are: ..DE, D.Columbia, FL [1], GA, SC, NC, VI, KY, IN [2], OH, MI [3], PA, NY, MA, RI, NJ, ME, VT, NH, CO, MD.
  • Central Standard, (CST), UT-6; States are: ..eastern ND, MO, MS, IL, eastern SD, IA, NE, MN, WI, TX, AR, TN, AL, LA
  • Mountain Standard, (MST), UT-7; States are: MT, ID, WY, western SD, western ND, AZ, NM, UT.
  • Pacific Standard, (PST), UT-8; States are: ..CA, WA (state), OR, NV.
  • Alaska Standard (AKST) is UT-9;   but at this latitude, a small distance east or west translates to an huge shift of noon-time, and in Nome, noon is at 3pm AKST; in Fairbanks, sunset in summer is after midnight AKST. In 1960, Alaska had three timezones, UT-8 round Juneau, UT-9 from Cross Sound to 141W, UT-10 from 141W to 162W. A base-time of UT+9, generally, came in on 30 Nov. 1983. Abolishing DST has been proposed, twice, since.

[3] In Michigan, the counties of Gogerbie, Iron, Dickinson & Menominee are in UT-6 zone.

U.S. Offshore, Hawaii and the Aleutian Isl. keep UT-10, Until Sept 1945, Hawaii's zone was UT-10.30 - (they had permanent wartime DST 9 Feb '42 to 30 Sep '45); Midway UT-11; American Samoa is UT-11; Panama UT-5 - all with no DST.
The Marshall Islands UT+12. Guam UT+10.
Puerto Rico is UT-5, and had DST only in 1941-'45.

Central America: In Mexico, and the rest of Central America (not Panama), the timezone is UT-6 (which is CST in US). (In 1960, Narayit, Sinaloa, the Southern district of Lower California and Sonora kept UT-7; the Northern district of Lower California kept UT-8.). Before recent times, Mexico's timezone history has been chequered: UT-7 before 1927, and after Nov 1930; after 16 Dec '43, UT-5. 1944 May 1 re-adopted UT-6. One modern map suggests that the NW state of Sonora keeps UT-7, while Baja Calif. Norte keeps UT-8. Mexico has various DST arrangements - [DST].
Others at UT-6 are: Costa Rica [DST] (may not be current), Guatemala [DST], Honduras [DST], and Nicaragua (not current) [DST], Belize, El Salvador.

Only Panama uses UT-5 [DST]

The West Indies are largely UT-4; this covers Anguilla, Antigua, Montserrat, Dominica, Barbados, Dominican Rep. (from UT-4.40' on 1 Apr 1933 to UT-5; 1974, oct 27, to UT-4) [DST], Grenada, Martinique, Guadeloupe, St Lucia, Virgin Isl., and Puerto Rico; Nevis & Kitts, Trinidad & Tobago. So too, Bermuda.

The following are UT-5:
The Bahamas [DST], Cuba [DST], Jamaica, Haiti, and the Cayman Is. - these all maintain UT-5. Turks & Caicos, Netherlands Antilles (Curacao), no DST, (in 1960 was UT-4.30).
Saint Pierre and Miquelon uses UT-3, [DST] (in 1960 was UT-3)

Russia & satellite states, after abandoning DST in 2010, have moved to new all-year times, across all zones, as of 28 Oct, 2011. (Moldova may do the same). [DST]

  • On the European side of Russia, Kalliningrad on the Baltic (formerly Konigsberg), and Belarus keep UT+3, which they call MSK-1.
  • Moscow zone (MSK time) is UT+4 now, and Samara, Saratov, Kazan, Volgograd and Voronezh, all also UT+4.
  • UT+5 is not used.
  • Yekaterinburg is the main city in zone UT+6, which includes Perm, Omsk, Ufa, Chelyabinsk.
  • Krasnoyarsk is UT+8. (Little habitation to the east of it!).
  • At the Pacific coast, Yakutsk is UT+10, Vladivostok is UT+11hrs. (with most of Sakhalin Oblast), Kamchatka is UT+12 (Magadan, eastern Sakha Republ.).
Old Russian times: Solar time (use LMT) was in use until late nineteenth century (1890s), when Moscow time was declared as UT+2.30. However, when the Soviet Union was created, Moscow Time became UTC+2 and the various other time zones were introduced throughout Russia and the rest of the Soviet Union.
On June 21, 1930, the Soviet Union advanced all clocks by one hour, so Moscow's MST became UT+3, and see other zones for the addition, right.

Africa has four timezones (and very few countries have had DST; only Algeria DST, Egypt [DST], Madagascar [DST], Morocco [DST], Namibia, Rep. South Africa [DST], Tunisia [DST], South Sudan (maybe), and Western Sahara, [DST], as far as I can find out, and some of these inconsistently. Tunisia abandoned it in 2009, Egypt in 2011, Madagascar, 1954, Rep. South Africa, 1944.

WEST: In Benin, Togo, Burkina Faso, Cote d'Ivoire, Sierre Leone, Liberia, Guinea, Senegal, Mauritania, Mali, also Morocco (which adopted UT in 1913, 26 oct, 00.30am.) , and Western Sahara, it's UT, (= GMT).

WESTERN CENTRAL: in a zone including Libya and Namibia, and west of a line between them towards the nations listed above, all countries use UT +1
- that's Algeria, (adopted UT 10 mar 1911; from 1900 — 1911 it was Paris Time, UTC +0:09:21; before that, use lmt) [DST], Nigeria (adopted UT+1 on 31 Aug 1919, and before that, use lmt), Niger, Benin, Cameroon & its islands, Equatorial Guinea & Annobon Is., Chad, Gabon, Central African Rep., Angola, Tunisia [DST], and the western Congo, (like Kinshasa).

EASTERN CENTRAL: east of that line from Libya to Namibia, and excepting those below, nations use UT+2
- Burundi, Democratic Rep of Congo (Kisangani, Katanga -eastern), Zambia, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Mozambique, down to Malawi and Rep. South Africa [DST]. Egypt [DST] and Libya are also on UT+2.

EAST: the countries near the east coast, from Sudan, South Sudan, Eritrea, Ethiopia, Somalia, Somaliland, Kenya, down to Uganda and Tanzania, with Madagascar (since 1911) [DST], and the Comoros Is., all use timezone UT+3.

Europe is mostly UT+1.

Dealing with the exceptions first:

  • Ukraine, the Baltic States of Estonia, Latvia & Lithuania, Finland, Romania, Bulgaria, Cyprus, Greece and Moldova are UT+2. (Greece briefly used UT+1.24)
  • The UK, and Eire [but Eire see footnote], use UT, as do Portugal, Iceland (in 1960, was UT-1), and, according to some sources, the islands of Ascension, Tristan da Cunha, St. Helena, and the Faeroe Islands. For 39 years to 1940 Spain also used UT as base timezone.
  • Note: the Azores (Port.) are UT-1.
  • Note: Belarus and Kallinin (Ru.) are UT+3.

States at UT+1 are: Albania, Andorra, Austria, Belgium, Bosnia & Herzegovina, Croatia, Czech Rep., France & Corsica, Germany, Gibraltar, Hungary, Italy & Sardinia & Sicily, Liechtenstein, Luxembourg, Macedonia, Malta, Monte Carlo, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, San Marino, Serbia, Slovenia, Slovakia, Spain & the Balearics (of 2014, Spain may revert to UT!), Switzerland.

DST: all states that are in the EU and use DST have standardised summertime shift-dates currently, though some have not ratified the E.U. Directive. These dates for DST are copied by states both near and far outside the Union, for practicality.

New Zealand now is 12 hours fast of UT. It has DST from October to April of the following year, and has observed it nearly ninety years.

The use of DST began 30 April 1916 with Germany and its World War I allies. Britain, most of its war allies, and some European neutrals soon did the same.

Australia
In compliance with a Commonwealth order, 1917 was the first year DST was used across the country, running 1 Jan to 25 Mar, through the southern hemisphere summer. It was discontinued after the war ended in 1918, but World War II saw its return for three consecutive summers, beginning with a late summer start on 1 Jan 1942, followed by full summers Sep to Mar.
"The Tasmanian state government implemented Daylight Savings in the summer of 1967 to save power which saved water. As it turned out, they liked having DST in Tasmania so much so that they have continued it ever since. Because it was such a success, the Tasmanian state government pushed to get DST used Australia-wide".

In 1971 Australia's first peace-time DST enactment was made. Although efforts have been made to unify DST dates across Australia, the Federal Government has kept it the responsibility of individual state and territory governments to decide when and if Daylight Savings will be implemented locally.
For example New South Wales extended DST in 1981-2 because of power shortages. During the Sydney 2000 Olympic Games special Daylight Savings Times were observed by some, but not all.

Canada
Six Canadian cities, by local ordinance, used DST before 1918: Brandon, Manitoba and Winnipeg (already in 1916), also Halifax, Hamilton, Ontario, Montreal, St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador.
Subsequently, Edmonton had DST in 1918, and it ran mid-Apr to late Oct, in 1919 mid-Apr to 27 May, in 1920 late Apr to September, through to 1923, when on Sept 30 DST was abandoned till 1942, in the war.
From 1942 to 30 Sep 1945, Summer-Time ran all year round. None in 1946; in 1947 DST ran for that year only.
At some point Saskatchewan voted to not use DST.

In 1967, DST ran for that year only, 30 Apr to 29 Oct.
At some point, Saskatchewan voted, for the greater part, to not use DST.

Since 1969, DST across Canada has more or less followed the U.S.: when the United States extended DST in 1987 to the first Sunday in April, all DST-observing Canadian provinces followed suit.
So, before the 2006 US act, Canada observed DST from the first Sunday in Apr. until the last Sunday in Oct.. The latest United States change (Energy Policy Act of 2005, adding parts of March and November, to start in 2007), was adopted by Canadian provinces and territories as follows:
Ontario, Manitoba - October 20, 2005
Quebec - December 5, 2005
Prince Edward Island - December 6, 2005
New Brunswick - December 23, 2005
Alberta - February 2, 2006
Northwest Territories - March 4, 2006
British Columbia - March 31, 2006
Nova Scotia - April 25, 2006
Yukon - July 14, 2006
Newfoundland - November 20, 2006, but officially announced on January 18, 2007 Nunavut - February 19, 2007

Currently, in most of Canada, DST begins at 2:00 a.m. local time on the second Sunday in Mar.; ends on the first Sunday in Nov., when areas return to Standard Time at 2:00 a.m..
Areas of Canada not using DST are very many: they include Fort St. John, Charlie Lake, Taylor and Dawson Creek in British Columbia, Creston in the East Kootenays, and most of Saskatchewan, except Denare Beach and Creighton.

Chile
Chile has used DST since summer 1927. At that time, the country was divided into two zones. The current law of 2008 applies to the whole country and provides that summer time starts at midnight on the last Saturday of October and again ends at midnight on the second Saturday of march. In some years, the data adjusted for political or climatic reasons, e.g. 2012, 2nd Sep - 29 Apr..
Historical exceptions:

  • In 1987 the time change to daylight saving time on Saturday was postponed until April 11 due to the visit of Pope John Paul II.
  • In 1988, DST began a week earlier in order to have more light for the National Plebiscite of October 5.
  • In 1990 the winter schedule was postponed until 17 March as Patricio Aylwin began his presidency on March 11.
  • In 1990, the start of daylight saving was held on September 15, in order to save energy due to unfavorable hydrological conditions.
  • In 1997, the start of the official time was on March 29 to meet adverse hydrological conditions.
  • In 2008 the return to standard time was delayed three weeks due to a severe drought that hit the country.
  • In 2010, due to the earthquake that rocked the country, the winter schedule began at 00:00 on April 4, (Easter Island at 20:00 the previous day).
  • In 2011 the government postponed the time change in the expectation of a dry year. After a first extension of DST for the entire month of March, the change of official time was scheduled for 7 may 24:00, and Easter Island at 22:00. Ended 19 aug.
  • In 2012 the government decided to keep the plan implemented in 2011 starting 28 apr, 24:00, and Easter Island at 22:00. Ended sep.

New Zealand
Starting in 1909, the Honourable Sir Thomas Kay Sidey put forward Bills to advance the clocks an hour, and The Summer Time Act 1927 brought it in: first Sunday in November to the first Sunday in March. This proved unpopular; the Summer Time Act 1928 revised this to a half-hour shift from the 14 October 1928 (second Sunday) to 17 March 1929 (third Sunday), and The Summer Time Act 1929 fixed this half-hour shift to run from the second Sunday in October to the third Sunday in March.
In 1933, the period was extended from the first Sunday in September to the last Sunday in April. This continued until the Second World War, when emergency regulations in 1941 extended daylight saving to cover the whole year, re-applied until the Standard Time Act of 1945 made the abandonment of NZMT permanent in 1946, so what had beencalled N.Z. Summer Time (NZST) became N.Z. Standard Time.
By the Time Act 1974, the Governor-General could declare a period when Daylight Time is to be observed. This was a one-hour shift (on top of the earlier half-hour shift) from the first Sunday in November to the last Sunday in February, promptly changed the next year by The New Zealand Time Order 1975 which fixed the period of observance from the last Sunday in October to the first Sunday in March.
AS a consequence of the survey and further feedback, in 1988 the Minister of Internal Affairs arranged for a trial of extended NZDT to be held from the second Sunday in October 1989 to the third Sunday in March 1990, and The Daylight Time Order 1990 declared that NZDT would run from 2 am NZST on the first Sunday in October to 3 am NZDT on the third Sunday in March.
On 30 April 2007 the government announced that it had extended the daylight saving period from 24 to 27 weeks. From September 2007, daylight saving is now observed from the last Sunday in September until the first Sunday in April.
From 30 April 2007, DST begins 02:00 NZST on the last Sunday in September, and ends at 03:00 NZDT (or 02:00 NZST as defined in the Time Act 1974) on the first Sunday in April.

U.S.A.
Beginning 1918 during the First World War, DST was voted down August of that year. It was federally re-instated in 1926 while allowing states much choice; most states west of the Mississippi, and those south of the Ohio, did not adopt it.

In the Second World War its general imposition ran Feb 9, 1942, to Sep 30, 1945, without a break - so all charts between those dates should be erected applying DST to the base-time.

In 1945 it reverted States' (or cities'!) choices until 1966, and during this time it was near universal in PE, MA, DE, and states to the north and east of those. Also DST was used in California and Nevada. But between, to quote Wikipedia, "In 1954, only a few cities between Nevada and St. Louis, Miss. had DST". By 1966, cities' discretionary adoptions had made the situation chaotic.
The Uniform Time Act of 13 Apr 1966 obliged each state to choose whether or not to adopt, though a state spanning in a neighbouring time-zone could opt that part out; commonly, the rule adopted was last Sunday in March; last Sunday in October (but there are exceptions - as an example, Alaska adopted 'last week April to last week October', from 1969 to 1986; 'first week April to last week October' after that).
Indiana and Alaska opted out, but soon re-joined the vast majority. But Arizona, which for a few months in 1967 had DST, has stayed at base-time all year ever since.
In 1974-5, the oil crisis caused the near year-long extension of DST; public opposition ended that long DST.

Finally, in March 11, 2007, DST was re-designated to run 2nd Sunday in March; first Sunday in November. Some states, like Indiana and Alaska, are considering abandoning DST again.
US offshore islands are listed separately.
